1 What the HDI Doesn't Show

Wednesday ~20 min

You already know that the HDI combines three dimensions: health (life expectancy), knowledge (years of schooling), and standard of living (GNI per capita). But there's a problem.

If a country's average income is high, but most of the money belongs to a tiny group of people — does the average really describe life there?

Think back to the Yemen example from last week: the GNI per capita was $910, but former President Saleh had a net worth of $64 billion while 82% of the population needed aid. The average disguised the reality.

The United Nations recognised this problem and created a second measurement:

The IHDI takes the same three HDI dimensions (health, education, income) and adjusts them based on how unequally they are distributed across the population.

If everyone in a country had exactly the same life expectancy, education, and income, the HDI and IHDI would be identical. But the more inequality there is, the lower the IHDI drops compared to the HDI.

The difference between the two numbers is called the "inequality penalty" — it tells you how much development is being lost to inequality.

Key idea: The HDI measures a country's potential human development. The IHDI measures the actual human development experienced by the average person.

The UNDP publishes an interactive IHDI explorer. You can look up any country and see how its score changes when inequality is accounted for.

Answer this before moving on.

Country X has an HDI of 0.850 and an IHDI of 0.620. What does this tell you?

That's right. The large gap (0.850 → 0.620 = 27% loss) means that while the country's averages look good, inequality means many people experience much less development than the headline number suggests.
Not quite. Look at the gap between the two numbers — 0.850 is quite high (good average development), but 0.620 is much lower. What could cause that difference? Think about what "inequality-adjusted" means. Try again.

2 Your Country's Inequality Penalty

Wednesday / Thursday ~30 min

Now apply this to the countries from your Week 1 country report. Below is a table with the HDI data you already know. Your job: look up the IHDI for each country and calculate the "loss" percentage.

How to calculate loss: Loss (%) = ((HDI − IHDI) ÷ HDI) × 100. For example, if HDI = 0.970 and IHDI = 0.909, the loss = ((0.970 − 0.909) ÷ 0.970) × 100 = 6.3%

3 The Equity Factor

Thursday ~25 min

Last week, you identified three factors of human development: Health, Knowledge, and Standard of Living. This week you've discovered a fourth:

New Factor
Equity — the fair distribution of health, education, and income across a population

Now you'll add this to your country report and practise a skill you'll need for your summative research paper: making a claim from evidence.

Your Task (this goes in your country report document)

1. Add an "Equity" section to your Week 1 country report.

2. Include the data: your country's HDI, IHDI, and the percentage loss.

3. Find one source that helps explain why your country has this level of inequality. Is it income inequality? Unequal access to education? Regional health disparities? Record:

  • What you found
  • Where you found it (URL or title)
  • How it helps explain the IHDI gap

4. Write a claim (2–3 sentences) answering:

Does the HDI fairly represent life in [your country]?

"The HDI [does / does not] fairly represent life in [country] because..."

"While [country]'s HDI of ___ suggests [high/medium/low] development, the IHDI of ___ reveals that..."

"The biggest source of inequality in [country] is ___, which means that the HDI overestimates / underestimates..."

A strong claim names the data (HDI and IHDI), identifies the cause of the gap, and makes a judgment about whether the HDI is a fair representation. A weak claim just restates the numbers without explaining what they mean.
